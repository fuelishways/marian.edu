Nina Mason Pulliam EcoLab

Nina Mason Pulliam EcoLab

A campus jewel, the 55-acre Nina Mason Pulliam EcoLab is located on the northwest end of campus near the Cycloplex.

Access to the EcoLab, located below a small bluff along Crooked Creek, is provided via an S-shaped stone stairway behind the university's historic Allison Mansion.

Mink, fox, muskrat, deer, beaver, turtles, and other mammals—in addition to 165 species of birds like hawks and herons—have been spotted in this habitat which includes two miles of well-marked walking trails.

The EcoLab is comprised of wetlands and a pond, a lowland forest and meadow filled with native trees, grasses, and plants, and a round, open-sided outdoor classroom. The pond serves as both a summer home and a winter lodge for beavers who store vegetation and sticks under the water on which to feed when the pond freezes.

Notice the stone cisterns and bridges that were part of Riverdale, the American country house estate of James Allison, a founder of the Indianapolis Motor Speedway. Designed by master landscape architect Jens Jensen in 1912, the cisterns collected spring water used for drinking, air conditioning, and irrigation in and around the mansion.

Known for his Prairie School style, Jensen was expert at incorporating native species in his designs, which included over 350 private estates during his career. Fewer than ten percent of his designs survive today. The EcoLab and grounds around Allison Mansion are among the best remaining examples of Jensen's work in the world.

The EcoLab's mission is to promote environmental education, ecology and ecological restoration, conservation, and preservation of natural habitats. It is a valuable teaching, learning, and research tool. A recent initiative involved finding and tagging box turtles to study their behavior in urban settings. One turtle (fondly named Arthur) is tagged with a radio transmitter so his movement, weight, breeding habits, and other data can be tracked.

The area contains over 260 types of plants, including many rare wetland species. Marian students, faculty, staff, and volunteers have devoted thousands of hours to reduce and eliminate invasive, non-native species from the area. EcoLab staff generally host a native plant sale once a year with dozens of different perennial plants, flowers, and grasses available for purchase.

Marian's EcoLab is used for field trips by K-12 teachers and school groups, summer camp youth, scouts, and others interested in the sciences. Staff offer a range of programming that's open to the general public, like night hikes to prowl for Great Horned Owls, tree identification and beekeeping workshops, and spring and fall wildflower walks
